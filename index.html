Q1.In a pollution study of 1500 Indian rivers, 520 were polluted by sulphur compounds, 335 by phosphates, 425 by crude oil, 100 by both crude oil and sulphur compounds, 180 by both sulphur compounds and phosphates, 150 by both phosphates and crude oil, and 28 by all three. How many rivers were polluted by at least one impurity? How many were not polluted by exactly one of the three? How many were not polluted at all?
Given
Total rivers $= 1500$
$|S| = 520,\ |P| = 335,\ |C| = 425$
$|S \cap C| = 100,\ |S \cap P| = 180,\ |P \cap C| = 150$
$|S \cap P \cap C| = 28$
Formula (Inclusion–Exclusion)
$|S \cup P \cup C| = |S|+|P|+|C| - |S\cap P| - |P\cap C| - |S\cap C| + |S\cap P\cap C|$
Part (a) — Polluted by at least one
$= 520 + 335 + 425 - 180 - 150 - 100 + 28$
$= 1280 - 430 + 28$
$= 878$
∴ Rivers polluted by at least one impurity $= \boxed{878}$
Part (b) — Not polluted by exactly one
Exactly one $= \sum |X| - 2\sum |X\cap Y| + 3|S\cap P\cap C|$
$= 1280 - 2(430) + 3(28)$
$= 1280 - 860 + 84 = 504$
Not polluted by exactly one $= 1500 - 504$
$= \boxed{996}$
Part (c) — Not polluted at all
$= 1500 - |S\cup P\cup C|$
$= 1500 - 878$
$= \boxed{622}$
Q2(1).Prove that $A \times (B \cap C) = (A \times B) \cap (A \times C)$.
Proof (Set equality by element)
Let $(x, y) \in A \times (B \cap C)$
$\iff x \in A \text{ and } y \in B \cap C$
$\iff x \in A \text{ and } (y \in B \text{ and } y \in C)$
$\iff (x \in A, y \in B) \text{ and } (x \in A, y \in C)$
$\iff (x, y) \in A \times B \text{ and } (x, y) \in A \times C$
$\iff (x, y) \in (A \times B) \cap (A \times C)$
∴ $A \times (B \cap C) = (A \times B) \cap (A \times C)$   ∎

(1) Find the number of people who read all three magazines. (2) Fill in the correct number in all regions of the Venn diagram. (3) Determine the number of people who read exactly one magazine.

$|N\cap T| = 9,\ |T\cap F| = 8,\ |N\cap F| = 9$ (read both N&F)

Part (1) — Solve for $x = |N\cap T\cap F|$
$52 = 25 + 26 + 26 - 9 - 8 - 9 + x$
$52 = 77 - 26 + x$
$52 = 51 + x$
$x = \boxed{1}$ person reads all three
Part (2) — Venn diagram regions
N only $= 25 - 9 - 9 + 1 = 8$
T only $= 26 - 9 - 8 + 1 = 10$
F only $= 26 - 8 - 9 + 1 = 10$
N∩T only $= 9 - 1 = 8$
T∩F only $= 8 - 1 = 7$
N∩F only $= 9 - 1 = 8$

$= 8 + 10 + 10$

Q4.Consider the set of integers from 1 to 500. (1) How many of these numbers are divisible by 3 or 5 or 11? (2) How many are divisible by 3 or by 11 but not by all of 3, 5 and 11? (3) How many are divisible by 3 or 11 but not by 5?
Given
$U = \{1, 2, \dots, 500\}$. Let $A,B,C$ = multiples of $3, 5, 11$.
Step 1 — Count individual multiples
$|A| = \lfloor 500/3 \rfloor = 166$
$|B| = \lfloor 500/5 \rfloor = 100$
$|C| = \lfloor 500/11 \rfloor = 45$
$|A\cap B| = \lfloor 500/15 \rfloor = 33$
$|A\cap C| = \lfloor 500/33 \rfloor = 15$
$|B\cap C| = \lfloor 500/55 \rfloor = 9$
$|A\cap B\cap C| = \lfloor 500/165 \rfloor = 3$

$|A\cup B\cup C| = 166 + 100 + 45 - 33 - 15 - 9 + 3$
$= 311 - 57 + 3$
$= \boxed{257}$

Subtract $|A\cap B\cap C| = 3$
$= 196 - 3$
$= \boxed{193}$

Q5.Each of the following defines a relation on the positive integers: (1) $xy$ is the square of an integer; (2) $x + y = 10$. Determine which relations are reflexive, symmetric, antisymmetric and/or transitive.
Part (1) — $xRy \iff xy$ is a perfect square
Reflexive: $x \cdot x = x^2$ → square ✓
Symmetric: $xy = yx$ → if $xy$ square then $yx$ square ✓
Transitive: if $xy = a^2$ and $yz = b^2$, then $xy \cdot yz = a^2 b^2$
$\Rightarrow y^2 \cdot xz = (ab)^2 \Rightarrow xz = (ab/y)^2$ → square ✓
Antisymmetric: $2\cdot 8 = 16$ (sq) and $8\cdot 2 = 16$ (sq), but $2 \neq 8$ ✗
∴ Reflexive, Symmetric, Transitive — Equivalence Relation
Part (2) — $xRy \iff x + y = 10$
Reflexive: needs $x + x = 10 \Rightarrow x = 5$ (only) ✗
Symmetric: $x + y = y + x = 10$ ✓
Antisymmetric: $3R7$ and $7R3$ but $3 \neq 7$ ✗
Transitive: $3R7,\ 7R3$ but $3 + 3 = 6 \neq 10$ ✗
∴ Only Symmetric
Q6.Prove that if $R$ is an equivalence relation on a set $A$, then $R^{-1}$ is also an equivalence relation.
Given
$R$ is reflexive, symmetric, transitive on $A$
$R^{-1} = \{(b, a) : (a, b) \in R\}$
Step 1 — Reflexive
$\forall a \in A,\ (a, a) \in R$ (given)
$\Rightarrow (a, a) \in R^{-1}$ ✓
Step 2 — Symmetric
Let $(a, b) \in R^{-1} \Rightarrow (b, a) \in R$
By symmetry of $R$: $(a, b) \in R$
$\Rightarrow (b, a) \in R^{-1}$ ✓
Step 3 — Transitive
Let $(a, b), (b, c) \in R^{-1}$
$\Rightarrow (b, a), (c, b) \in R \Rightarrow (c, b), (b, a) \in R$
By transitivity of $R$: $(c, a) \in R$
$\Rightarrow (a, c) \in R^{-1}$ ✓
∴ $R^{-1}$ is reflexive, symmetric, transitive ⇒ Equivalence Relation ∎
Q7.Check if $\mathbb{N}$ with the “divides” relation is a POSet.
Reflexive
$\forall a \in \mathbb{N},\ a \mid a$ (since $a = a \cdot 1$) ✓
Antisymmetric
If $a \mid b$ and $b \mid a$ for $a, b \in \mathbb{N}$
$\Rightarrow b = ka,\ a = mb \Rightarrow a = mka \Rightarrow mk = 1$
$\Rightarrow m = k = 1 \Rightarrow a = b$ ✓
Transitive
If $a \mid b$ and $b \mid c$: $b = ka,\ c = mb$
$\Rightarrow c = m(ka) = (mk)a \Rightarrow a \mid c$ ✓
∴ $(\mathbb{N}, |)$ is a POSet ∎

Q11.If $\mathbb{R}$ is the set of real numbers, show that the function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f(x) = 5x^3 - 1$ is a one-one onto function.
One-One (Injective)
Assume $f(a) = f(b)$
$\Rightarrow 5a^3 - 1 = 5b^3 - 1$
$\Rightarrow 5a^3 = 5b^3$
$\Rightarrow a^3 = b^3$
$\Rightarrow a = b$ ✓ (cube root is unique in $\mathbb{R}$)
Onto (Surjective)
Let $y \in \mathbb{R}$. Solve $y = 5x^3 - 1$ for $x$
$\Rightarrow x^3 = \dfrac{y + 1}{5}$
$\Rightarrow x = \sqrt[3]{\dfrac{y+1}{5}} \in \mathbb{R}$
Check: $f(x) = 5\cdot\dfrac{y+1}{5} - 1 = y$ ✓
∴ $f$ is one-one and onto ⇒ Bijection ∎

Assignment 2 — Counting, Recursion & Induction

Q1.Define: (1) Recurrence relation, (2) Well-Ordering Principle, (3) Pigeonhole Principle.
(1) Recurrence Relation
An equation that defines each term of a sequence as a function of one or more previous terms.
Example: $a_n = a_{n-1} + a_{n-2}$ with $a_0 = 0,\ a_1 = 1$ (Fibonacci).
(2) Well-Ordering Principle
Every non-empty subset $S \subseteq \mathbb{N}$ has a least element.
i.e. $\exists m \in S$ such that $m \le x,\ \forall x \in S$.
(3) Pigeonhole Principle
If $n + 1$ objects are placed into $n$ boxes, at least one box contains $\ge 2$ objects.
Generalised: with $N$ objects in $k$ boxes, some box has $\ge \lceil N/k \rceil$ objects.
Q2.How many passwords can be created using 5 letters from the alphabet?
Given
Length $= 5$,  alphabet size $= 26$,  repetition allowed.
Formula
$N = 26^5$ (Product rule)
Step-by-Step
$26^2 = 676$
$26^3 = 676 \times 26 = 17{,}576$
$26^4 = 17{,}576 \times 26 = 456{,}976$
$26^5 = 456{,}976 \times 26 = 11{,}881{,}376$
∴ $N = \boxed{11{,}881{,}376}$ passwords
Q3.Give a recursive definition for the factorial function $f(n) = n!$, then find $f(5)$.
Recursive Definition
Base case: $f(0) = 1$
Recursive step: $f(n) = n \cdot f(n-1),\ \forall n \ge 1$
Compute $f(5)$ step-by-step
$f(1) = 1 \cdot f(0) = 1 \cdot 1 = 1$
$f(2) = 2 \cdot f(1) = 2 \cdot 1 = 2$
$f(3) = 3 \cdot f(2) = 3 \cdot 2 = 6$
$f(4) = 4 \cdot f(3) = 4 \cdot 6 = 24$
$f(5) = 5 \cdot f(4) = 5 \cdot 24 = 120$
∴ $f(5) = \boxed{120}$
Q4.How many ways are there to select six players from a 10-member tennis team to make a trip to a match at another college?
Formula (Combinations)
$C(n, r) = \dfrac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}$
Substitute $n = 10,\ r = 6$
$C(10, 6) = \dfrac{10!}{6! \cdot 4!}$
$= \dfrac{10 \cdot 9 \cdot 8 \cdot 7}{4!}$
$= \dfrac{5040}{24}$
$= 210$
∴ Number of ways $= \boxed{210}$
Q5.A company has 30 employees, 10 are assigned to Task A, 15 to Task B, and 5 are assigned to both. How many employees are assigned to at least one task?
Given
$|A| = 10,\ |B| = 15,\ |A \cap B| = 5$
Formula
$|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|$
Substitute
$= 10 + 15 - 5$
$= 20$
∴ Employees on at least one task $= \boxed{20}$
Q6.Find $\gcd(231, 1820)$ using the Euclidean algorithm.
Algorithm: $\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b,\ a \bmod b)$
$1820 = 7 \cdot 231 + 203$   (since $7 \cdot 231 = 1617$, $1820 - 1617 = 203$)
$231 = 1 \cdot 203 + 28$
$203 = 7 \cdot 28 + 7$   (since $7 \cdot 28 = 196$, $203 - 196 = 7$)
$28 = 4 \cdot 7 + 0$
Last non-zero remainder $= 7$
∴ $\gcd(231, 1820) = \boxed{7}$
Q7.There are 40 male and 30 female faculties in a college. The principal wants to select one faculty (either male or female) for examination duty. In how many ways can this selection be done?
Given
Males $= 40,\ $ Females $= 30$

Compute
$= 40 + 30$

Q9.Use mathematical induction to prove that $7(2^n) + 2(3^{n-3}) \cdot 3^{(n-1)}$ is divisible by 25 for $n \in \mathbb{N}$.

Q1.Explain syntax vs semantics with formal examples.

Refers to the form — rules for building well-formed formulas.

Semantics assigns each propositional variable a truth value $T$ or $F$.

Q6.State basic laws of logic.
Idempotent: $p \vee p \equiv p$,  $p \wedge p \equiv p$
Commutative: $p \vee q \equiv q \vee p$,  $p \wedge q \equiv q \wedge p$
Associative: $(p \vee q) \vee r \equiv p \vee (q \vee r)$
Distributive: $p \wedge (q \vee r) \equiv (p \wedge q) \vee (p \wedge r)$

Complement: $p \vee \neg p \equiv T$,  $p \wedge \neg p \equiv F$
De Morgan: $\neg(p \vee q) \equiv \neg p \wedge \neg q$

Implication: $p \to q \equiv \neg p \vee q$
∴ These nine laws form the foundation of propositional algebra
